# cirrus — references

Research pass for the *Cirrus* zoetrope piece. URLs marked
"verify" haven't been visited live; the steal is the load-bearing
content, not the citation.

## 1. Cyriak Harris — *Cirrus* music video (Bonobo, 2013)

Cyriak's official video for the track. Found-footage stop-motion
collage built from Prelinger Archives stock footage (American
thrift-era industrial films); single frames are stabilised, then
cycled, mirrored, replicated through space. Forms emerge from the
*alignment* of repeated cells across rotational symmetries —
groups of dancers become a single pulsing organism when the cell
period syncs to a Bonobo loop period.

Steal: forms made visible by *synchronisation* of cyclic cells.
A tooth alignment is more readable than a tooth in motion.

URL (verify): <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WF34N4gJAKE>

## 2. Charles-Émile Reynaud — Praxinoscope (1877)

Cylinder of cyclic images viewed via an inner ring of tilted
mirrors at half the radius. Unlike the zoetrope's slits (lossy,
flickery), the praxinoscope's central mirror keeps each image
*perceptually stationary* during its viewing instant — a mechanical
lock between rotation and visibility.

Steal: the centre of the piece must hold still while the rings
rotate around it. That's what `hub-bloom` is for in the layer
DAG. Stillness at origin = perceptual lock for the rotating
periphery.

URL: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Praxinoscope>

## 3. Eadweard Muybridge — Zoopraxiscope (1879)

Two rotating disks: one with sequential photos around its rim, one
with viewing slots. Motion appears when the slot/image period ratio
is right; mismatch produces stuttering or reverse motion. The
*beat* of the two rotations is the device's whole language —
2:1, 3:2, etc., produce different motion qualities.

Steal: pairs of adjacent rings form their own beat patterns. A
viewer who only watches rings 2 and 3 (P=11 and P=13) sees a
slow rotation period of LCM(11,13)/13 = 11 cycles of ring 3 per
alignment with ring 2. That's a 5.5-second meta-beat at 2 bars/cycle
— audible as a polyrhythmic figure if the audio is doing similar
work.

URL: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoopraxiscope>

## 4. Moiré beat math — coprime rotations

Two gratings with periods *a* and *b* where gcd(a,b)=1 overlapping
and rotating produce interference fringes that *themselves rotate*
at a beat frequency. The fringes are wider and slower than the
underlying gratings, which is the visual hook: the moiré is more
salient than the cause.

Tooth counts {5, 7, 11, 13, 17} are pairwise coprime; LCM is
85,085. At a base rotation rate of 1 rev/8 s for the slowest ring,
total alignment of all 5 rings happens once per ~10⁵ seconds —
practically never. Pairwise alignments happen every few seconds.
The piece lives in the pairwise-alignment regime; total alignment
is the never-arrived destination.

URL: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moiré_pattern>

## 5. Polyrhythm visualisers — clocks-as-rhythm

Tools like 4four.io's "Ploop" and various polyrhythm-visualiser web
apps draw beats as rotating clock hands at integer-tooth counts.
Watching a 5-vs-7 clock visualise is exactly the "almost-aligned"
prediction-engagement Kaplan describes for mystery — the eye
keeps placing the next click and being almost-right.

Steal: this is the V-Jaygent piece's *thesis* compressed to a
toy. The piece must be richer than the toy (warm palette, layered
strata, audio coupling, cursor + keyboard input) but the toy's
core engagement loop is already proven.

URL (verify): <https://4four.io/ploop>

## 6. Leif Podhajsky — *The North Borders* artwork (Bonobo, 2013)

Album art for the source track. Geometric repeat motifs with
analogue texture. Each variant of the layout is the same template
rotated through different colour assignments — exactly the kind
of symmetry-with-perturbation the piece wants visually.

V-Jaygent's palette stays warm regardless of the source's cool
artwork (gold → amber → wine, per VISION). The structural lesson
is the symmetry-with-variation, not the colour.

URL (verify): <https://leifpodhajsky.com/Bonobo-The-North-Borders>

## Distilled pattern

Every reference here shares one move: **forms become visible at
moments of synchronisation between cyclic processes**. Reynaud's
mirror locks the frame; Muybridge's slots gate the photo; Cyriak's
collage lines up dancers into a creature; the moiré is the
fringe-beat of two gratings; the clock visualiser is the alignment
of two periodic clicks.

The piece's job: make those alignments *brightenings* — when
multiple rings have a tooth at the same azimuth, the radial line
through them blooms warm. Cursor wind disrupts the sync; key
presses insert deliberate asymmetries. The whole composition is
the dance between alignment and perturbation.
