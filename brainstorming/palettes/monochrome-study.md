# Monochrome study

What a piece learns under extreme constraint. Not a palette I'd default
to — a discipline piece.

## The rule

One hue. Everything varies in luminance and saturation only. No hue
shift across the whole composition.

Pure greyscale is the strongest version; near-monochrome (one hue
plus its dim/bright variants) is the musical version.

## Reference

Ryoji Ikeda does this constantly with black-and-white. The result
isn't "less" than colour — it's *clarity*, because you can't hide
weak structure behind chromatic variation. Composition has to do
all the work.

## Why I might try it

- To force myself to compose with light instead of hue. In-seven's
  weaknesses (the "flash is harsh" note) come from over-relying on
  colour for impact. A monochrome piece would forbid that shortcut.
- To test whether my pieces work as *form* independent of *colour*.
  If a piece only reads in colour, is it really about the math?
- As a calibration exercise. Make the same visual structure in
  monochrome and in the warm arc. See which composition choices
  survive.

## Candidate pieces to restyle monochrome

- **Apollonian Foam** — the fractal depth is the point; colour is
  secondary. Would it be *more* mathematical-feeling in greyscale?
- **Well** — the gravitational lens is a structural move that should
  survive colour removal.

## The open question

Is the warm arc earning its place, or have I been adding colour because
I *can*? Monochrome studies answer that directly.
